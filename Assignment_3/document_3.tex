	\section{Problem Statement}
	If $A = \myvec{3 & -2 \\ 4 & -2}$ and $I = \myvec{1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1}$, find k so that $A^2 = kA - 2I$
	\section{Solution}
	\begin{align}
	Given : A &= \myvec{3 & -2 \\ 4 & -2},\\
	     A^2 &= AA \\
		&=  \myvec{3 & -2 \\ 4 & -2} \myvec{3 & -2 \\ 4 & -2}\\
		&= \myvec{9 - 8 & -6 + 4\\ 12 - 8 & -8 + 4}\\
		&= \myvec{1 & -2 \\ 4 & -4} \label{eq:res_1}
	\end{align}
\begin{align}
kA - 2I &= k\myvec{3 & -2 \\ 4 & -2} - 2\myvec{1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1}\\
&= \myvec{3k & -2k \\ 4k & -2k} - \myvec{2 & 0 \\ 0 & 2}\\
&= \myvec{3k - 2 & -2k \\ 4k & -2k - 2} \label{eq:res_2}
\end{align}	
Considering the given equation: $A^2 = kA - 2I$, from \eqref{eq:res_1} and \eqref{eq:res_2} we get
\begin{align}
	\myvec{1 & -2\\ 4 & -4} = \myvec{3k - 2 & -2k \\ 4k & -2k - 2}
\end{align}
Hence, k can be obtained by comparing the respective elements in both the matrices. i.e.,
\begin{align}
1 &= 3k - 2\\
\implies 1 + 2 &= 3k\\
\implies k &= 1
\end{align}
